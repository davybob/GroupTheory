2.

Symmetries
Ramble Before we begin our formal analysis of groups we should get an intuitive sense of what a group is. The symmetries of geometrical shapes provide an ideal start to this intuition. We will start by studying the symmetries of a square and then observing how this can be expanded to symmetries of any sized polygon.
The Square Imagine a coloured cardboard square laid on a plane with fixed axes, so that the centre of the square falls on the origin. See Figure 1.1.

Fig 1.1

We can see by observation that the square has rotational symmetry, (90°, 180°, or 270° rotation, we still get a square!) and reflexive symmetry (horizontal, vertical, topright-bottomleft, or topleft-bottomright flip, we still get a square!)
Symbols Let us formally define these symmetries as such:
Symmetry Image Description
I Original square
R 90° rotation
R' 180° rotation
R'' 270° rotation
H Horizontal flip
V Vertical flip
D Main diagonal flip
D' Other diagonal flip

What would happen, if we were to, oh say, flip horizontally then rotate the square 90°. Visually we see that this 'multiplication' of motions is equivalent to one single motion (Can you see which one?)
This is no coincidence, infact any two motions when applied one after another ("composed motions") yields a previously seen motion. (No new motions can be created from our set of motions. This property is known as closure and is one of the four requirements for a system to be a group, but more on this later!)
Algebra Algebraically we can write this composed motion, using our previously defined symbols as:
HR = D (The net effect on the vertices are the same!)
What if we applied the motion in reverse (rotate then flip)? Let's look at this motion visually

Algebraically this is written as
RH=D'
This is interesting, HR ≠ RH. (We say that our composed motion 'multiplication' is NOT commutative) When a system has the property that HR = RH we call it abelian. (We will see system with this property and their importance!)
Let's reverse back a bit, earlier I wrote that our composed motions are closed, but I did not prove this property. There are two ways about proving my proposition;
1. Create a Cayley Table
or
2. Arguementative.
In this section we will prove closure using 1. Later we will come back and prove using 2.* (If you can't wait that long, follow astrix).
Cayley table A Cayley table is a multiplication table for a group. It is a convienent method of showing all possible multiplications in the system. Most (3/4) of the group properties can be verified on the table. It is named after Arthur Cayley a prolific British Mathematician who first created this table. The Cayley table for the square is given below.
I R R' R'' H V D D'
I I R R' R'' H V D D'
R R R' R'' I D' D H V
R' R' R'' I R V H D' D
R'' R'' I R R' D D' V H
H H D V D' I R' R R''
V V D' H D R' I R'' R
D D V D' H R'' R I R'
D' D' H D V R R'' R' I

We can staight away see that any two composed motions creates an already seen motion (the table is full). We say that the system is closed. Algebraically we write
a,b ∈ G then ab ∈ G (where G is our system)
There is one particular motion that leaves the square as is; An identity motion I, this is the second property required for a group.
∀a, aI = Ia = a
Finally every motion is paired with another motion that yields the identity motion; An inverse motion exists, this is the third property required for a group.
Ǝa-1, a-1a = aa-1 = I
Although it is not evident from the table, the system is associative. We could show this but that would require calculating all 83 = 512 possible combinations of a, b, and c. *Easier to note that function composition is associative; a,b,c ∈ G then a(bc) = (ab)c)
Dihedral Groups Now that we have a good starting example we could we apply this procedure to an equilaterial triangle or a regular pentagon or, indeed, any regular n-gon (n >= 3). The corresponding group is denoted by Dn and is called the dihedral group of order 2n. (The order of a group being the number of elements in that group, we can see the symmetries of any n-gon have n rotational symmetries nad n reflexive symmetries).

The dihedral groups arise frequently in art and nature. Many decorative designs used on pottery, buildings and floor coverings, have one of the dihedral groups as a group of symmetry. Corporate logos are a good source of dihedral groups (E.G Mercedes-Benz). Even molecules have groups of symmetry (Look at ammonia NH3, it is equivalent to D3).
